So, there's a story I frequently tell about my first "real" job.  I've thought about a pithy title for this, including something like "Efficiency is a Lie" or something otherwise provocative, but I'm not sure I believe that enough to use it.  That said, I'd like you to keep AI in mind as I tell this story.  I'll return to it at the end, but I hope you see the through-line.

## The Story

### Before My Time

Once upon a time, in an office far, far away, there was a salaryman.  This salaryman had one real duty, which took him a solid 8 hours to complete.  As a salaryman, he wasn't allowed overtime, but he was also squarely instructed to keep his butt in his seat for 8 hours a day.

He quickly found that 8 hours of work turned into 7, into 6, into less.  However, to placate the Powers That Be, butts had to remain in seats.  As such, he searched for an activity to do.  There was a computer, and the salaryman had access to it.  On this computer was Solitaire, and from this computer Solitaire could not be removed, for computer modifications were banned by the Powers That Be.

So Solitaire consumed the remaining time.  First one game, then two.  Then one hour, then two.  No rules prevented this, so the Powers That Be were placated, even as others in the office gave the stink eye.

The decades rolled by.  No promotions came, but neither did PIPs.  The salaryman grew in efficiency, and the Solitaire time grew as well, to over 7 hours a day.  Eventually the salaryman retired, and a job was left vacant.

### So I Enter The Scene

In rolls a bright eyed, bushy tailed me (I know, indistinguishable from the current me).  I'm hired by this office, and given one primary duty:

- Take one newspaper from each of GA, NC, SC, AL, VA
- Take scissors, and cut any articles related to a select topic out of said newspaper
- Take a gluestick, and paste the articles into a spital notebook
- At the end of the day, fax the articles to the head office

That's it.  Oh, and if the spiral notebook fills, get a new one.
I got to work!  Tons of arts and crafts, scissoring, gluing, and faxing.  But one problem kept arising - arts and crafts for 8 hours a day really hurt my hands!

### So I Start Making Some Changes

So at this point, my hands really hurt.  Meanwhile, I'm a bright eyed, bushy tailed Millenial, and there's a computer with my account on it.  I'm not technical at this point, but I do know how to use a computer.  As proscripted by The Powers That Be, I must not make any modifications to the computer.  But lucky for me, there's some basic business software installed by default!  Microsoft Word, Microsoft Excel, and most importantly Microsoft SQL.

So at this point I go: "Hmmm.  Pasting articles into a spiral notebook.  Sharing that notebook via fax.  Sounds a lot like a database to me?," and promptly set up a database version of my spiral notebook.  By this point, I'm getting a bit better at scissors and glue, so even with double duty I'm done in time.  In fact, given that copy/paste from a newspaper's website exists, I'm clearing 7 hours of crafts in about 20 minutes of excited Googling.

This gives me spare time to improve my system.  What if you had tags by state?  What if you had tags by topic?  And so on.

### So Things Accelerate

So at this point, my hands still hurt, but I see an off-ramp.

One day, I go to my boss (one of the Powers That Be), and say to him "Hey Boss!  You mentioned to me the other day, how it would be great to see what's happening by state.  I built this nifty little tool here - I made these nice charts you can show to your boss!".  Of course, Boss wanted to make sure that No Rules Were Broken, but this was A OK! I was observant enough to realize that No Rules Were Broken was very very important.

Boss takes this to His Boss in the Head Office, and the Head Office is pleased as punch that he created this system.  So pleased, that he got a promotion, large raise, and an abduction back to the Head Office, bringing in a New Boss.

New Boss (and other Powers That Be) promptly realize that while I'm non-technical, I'm significantly more technical than anyone else, including them.  This helps them realize that with my help, they also might get a nice promotion and abduction to the Head Office.  Meanwhile, the Head Office is busy rolling with the database I built, and it starts spreading as the new Way Things Are Done Around Here.

To my great satisfaction, scissors and glue are put down (almost permanently, they still want a fax from time to time for old times' sake).  To my great chagrin, my job duties explode rather aggressively.  No opportunities for Solitaire are available to me.  To my great excitement, most of these duties are at least technical adjacent.

### The Rest Is History

As for me, I went on to use this opportunity to become technical, and take an engineering role at a startup.  My rising tide lifted many boats, leading to success, promotions, and Head Office abductions for many of the Powers That Be. To my knowledge, the era of scissors and glue permanently closed, as my hacked-together database went global.  To my knowledge, it is still in use today, and is the new scissors and glue of a new crop of probably-Gen-Alpha folks in suits and ties.

Many more stories were had from this workplace, but those are fables for another time.

## Hey Wait, Isn't This About AI?

I promised AI, and you'll get it now.  Notice its conspicuous absence, as it didn't exist at this time.

I want you to ask yourself - if this workplace went all-in on AI, what would happen?

First, it wouldn't, since the Powers That Be were all late adopters.  Installing anything new on any computer was such a hurdle, the implementation gains could never be worth the squeeze.  That's also neglecting reputational risk from any of them starting a project and watching it fail.

Second, if they did, it would not be adopted.  There were no incentives to do anything new in this organization.  As you might have noticed, The Powers That Be absorbed any gains made by their employees.  Fail, and there's no pip. Succeed, and someone else profits.  The economic incentive is to just sit on your laurels doing the bare minimum - and that's also explicitly what the rules ask for!  It took someone young and politically dumb (and with clumsy hands in this case) to look for gain within the system.

Third, even if it was adopted, it would be ineffective.  Information existed on paper and via word of mouth.  It took systemic computerization to reach a point where even decades-old tech was viable.  For AI to help, or even to act, it needs processes, practices, and permissions - information at hand, and access to it.  In an environment like this, it could have none of that.

I'm sure you're asking "What does this have to do with me and my startup?".  Well, quite a bit.  First, does your organization have an appetite for risk?  Second, do you have a culture of innovation?  Third, do you have a foundation of process that AI can accelerate?  If you don't have all three, you can certainly still pay for AI, and access AI.  But without all 3, your AI adoption is handicapped.  You get all of the cost at a fraction of the benefit.

Many companies I see are more like this story than not (and more than they'd like to admit).  Small doesn't necessarily mean scrappy.  Success requires you to hire the right people, build the right culture, and implement the right practices.  The alternative is to stay stuck in an era of scissors and glue.
